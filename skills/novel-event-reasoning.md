# Novel Event Reasoning — First-Principles Financial Analysis

## When to Use
When input falls outside all pre-defined categories. No template exists → reason from first principles.

## Core: Follow the Money

### Step 1: Identify Money Flow
| Flow Type | Question | Example (Olympics) |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Broadcasting | Who has rights? Ad revenue? | NBC/CMCSA ~$1B |
| Sponsorship | Who pays for brand association? | KO, PG, V — cost, not revenue |
| Tourism/Travel | Where do people go? | BKNG, ABNB, airlines |
| Betting/Gaming | Can people bet? Who facilitates? | DKNG, FLUT |
| Merchandise | What do people buy? | NKE — athlete gear |
| Content/Streaming | Where do people watch? | Peacock subscriber catalyst |
| Attention/Ad-tech | Who sells ads? | GOOGL, TTD |

### Step 2: Size the Revenue Impact
- <0.5% of company annual revenue → negligible
- 0.5%-2% → minor catalyst, narrative-driven, 1-2 weeks
- >2% → material catalyst, can drive earnings revision
- For mega-caps ($500B+), even $1B events are often <0.5%

### Step 3: Priced-In Check
**Scheduled events are almost ALWAYS priced in.** Calendar event = first-order impact priced in.
The trade is in DEVIATION from expectations (viewership beats, sponsors pull out, event disrupted).

### Step 4: Find Non-Obvious Angle
- Supply chain adjacents (who supplies the broadcasters?)
- Behavioral shifts (does event change spending patterns for weeks after?)
- Contrarian (if everyone says "buy X", X is probably already up)
- Duration (1-day event < 2-week event < permanent shift)

## Anti-Patterns
- ❌ "Olympics → buy Nike" (sponsorship is a cost, not revenue)
- ❌ $500M event = material for $100B company (do the math)
- ❌ Ignoring "priced in" for scheduled events
- ❌ Building 5-step chains when first-principles money-flow is simpler
